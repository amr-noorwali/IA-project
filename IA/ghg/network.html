network Information

A computer network is a system of interconnected devices, such as computers and servers, that enables the sharing of data and resources. Networks come in various forms, including Local Area Networks(LANs) within a confined area, Wide Area Networks (WANs) spanning larger geographic regions, and wireless networks (WLANs) utilizing technologies like Wi-Fi. Key components include nodes (devices), links (communication pathways), routers managing traffic, and switches facilitating efficient data transfer. Protocols like TCP/IP form the backbone of communication, defining how data is transmitted across the network. Different topologies, such as bus, star, ring, and mesh, dictate how devices are interconnected. Security measures, including firewalls, encryption, and Virtual Private Networks (VPNs), safeguard against unauthorized access. The Internet, a global network based on these principles, connects diverse networks worldwide, fostering communication, collaboration, and the exchange of information and services.

Internet

The Internet itself is a global network that connects millions of computers and networks worldwide, allowing information and data to be exchanged globally.

Definition:

computer network is a collection of interconnected devices, such as computers, servers, routers, and switches, that are capable of sharing data and resources. Networks can be classified based on their size, scope, and purpose.

Types of Networks:

Local Area Network (LAN): A LAN is a network limited to a small geographic area, such as a single building or campus. Computers and devices within a LAN can communicate with each other at high speeds.

Wide Area Network (WAN): A WAN covers a larger geographical area and typically connects multiple LANs. The Internet is an example of a global WAN.

Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN): WLANs use wireless communication to connect devices within a limited area, often using Wi-Fi technology.

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN): A MAN covers a larger geographic area than a LAN but is smaller than a WAN, typically serving a city or a large campus.

Personal Area Network (PAN): A PAN is a network for personal devices, typically within the range of an individual person, like connecting a smartphone to a laptop via Bluetooth.



Network Components:

Nodes: Devices connected to the network, such as computers, servers, printers, and other electronic devices.

Links: The communication pathways that connect nodes. Links can be wired (e.g., Ethernet cables) or wireless (e.g., Wi-Fi).

Router: Manages data traffic between different networks. Routers operate at the network layer of the OSI model.

Switch: Connects devices within the same network, enabling efficient data transfer. Switches operate at the data link layer.

Hub: Older technology that connects devices in a LAN but operates at the physical layer without the intelligence of a switch.

Protocols

Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol (TCP/IP): The fundamental suite of protocols that underlies the Internet and most modern networks.

Ethernet: A common LAN protocol that defines how data packets are placed on the network.

Wi-Fi (802.11): A set of standards for wireless local network communication..




DNetwork Topologies:

  • Bus: All devices share a single communication line..

  • Star: All devices are connected to a central hub or switch.

  • Ring: Devices are connected in a circular fashion

  • Mesh Devices are interconnected, providing multiple paths for data.

Top Cybersecurity Threats

Networks face various threats that can compromise their integrity, confidentiality, and availability. Common threats to networks include:

Threat Description
Password Attacks Techniques like brute-force attacks, where an attacker systematically tries all possible passwords, or password sniffing can compromise user credentials..
Insider Threats Malicious actions or negligence by individuals within an organization can pose a significant risk, as insiders may have access to sensitive data and systems..
Ransomware This type of malware encrypts data on a victim's system, demanding payment for its release. Ransomware attacks can have severe consequences for both individuals and organizations..
Social Engineering Techniques such as manipulating individuals into divulging sensitive information or exploiting trust relationships can be used to gain unauthorized access.
